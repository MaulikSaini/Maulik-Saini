## Case Study: The WannaCry Ransomware Attack (2017)

### What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software that encrypts a victim's files or locks them out of their system. The attacker then demands a ransom — usually in cryptocurrency — in exchange for restoring access.

---

### How It Happened (Step-by-Step)

1. **Initial Exploit:** WannaCry exploited a Windows vulnerability called **EternalBlue**, originally discovered by the NSA and later leaked online.
2. **Propagation:** Once one system was infected, the malware automatically scanned the network and spread to other vulnerable machines — no user clicks required.
3. **Encryption:** All files on infected systems were encrypted and renamed with a `.WNCRY` extension, making them completely inaccessible.
4. **Ransom Demand:** A pop-up message demanded **$300–$600 in Bitcoin** within 72 hours, threatening permanent data deletion if unpaid.
5. **Global Spread:** Within a single day, over **230,000 computers** across 150 countries were infected.

---

### Who Was Targeted?

- Hospitals and healthcare systems (notably the **UK's NHS**)
- Telecom companies (e.g., Spain's Telefónica)
- Government agencies and universities
- Any organization running **unpatched Windows XP or Windows 7** systems

---

### Consequences

- The **NHS alone** had to cancel over 19,000 appointments, costing an estimated **£92 million**.
- Global damages were estimated at **$4–8 billion USD**.
- Sensitive patient records and operational data were at risk.
- Governments worldwide issued emergency security patches and alerts.
- The attack was later attributed to **North Korea's Lazarus Group** by the US, UK, and Australia.

---

### Key Lesson

Keeping systems updated and regularly backing up data are the two most effective defenses against ransomware. WannaCry could have been prevented entirely with a single security patch released by Microsoft two months before the attack.
